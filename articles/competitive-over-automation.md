# Competitive Over-Automation and Demand Externalities.

The recent paper by Hemenway Falk and Tsoukalas formalizes a structural market failure called the demand externality. When a firm replaces a human worker with artificial intelligence, it captures the entire labor cost saving, denoted as *s* in the paper. The displaced worker loses income, which reduces aggregate expenditure, denoted as *D*, in the product market. Because the worker spent money across the entire sector, the firm that automated bears only a fraction of this lost revenue, specifically 1/*N* where *N* is the number of competing firms. The rest of the revenue loss falls on rival firms. This asymmetry creates an automation arms race. Rational firms systematically automate excessively, destroying aggregate consumer demand and ultimately harming both worker income and their own owner surplus.

Evaluating policy responses requires examining the marginal incentive to automate. Universal Basic Income acts as an unconditional transfer that increases baseline autonomous demand, denoted as *A*. UBI raises the profit floor for all firms but leaves the underlying marginal incentive to automate completely unchanged. A firm still makes the exact same mathematical calculation to replace humans. A Pigouvian automation tax targets the structural market failure directly. It levies a per task charge equal to the uninternalized demand loss a firm imposes on its competitors. This forces the firm to internalize the true cost of its decision, realigning private incentives with the collectively optimal automation rate.

These theoretical economic solutions fail when evaluated against rigid physical constraints. The model relies on a single isolated sector and evaluates policy based on mathematical incentives. Reality dictates that the majority of human capital is allocated to rent and housing in high population areas. Demand in these regions is high, and physical supply cannot easily scale. This inelasticity creates a massive spending leakage from mass consumer product markets. In the model, the parameter lambda represents the fraction of worker income spent within the sector. High housing costs cause lambda to shrink. When workers lose jobs, physical supply constraints prevent them from simply relocating or affording basic needs during transition periods. Retraining programs and tax interventions are expensive over time and lack the flexibility a physical society requires. Correcting a mathematical demand externality via a Pigouvian tax or subsidizing demand via UBI addresses abstractions in an isolated sector. These interventions do not solve the physical mismatch between supply and demand that governs actual living conditions in inelastic markets.
