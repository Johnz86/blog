# The Case for Deflationism: Why Language Models Are Just Next Token Predictors.

The debate on artificial intelligence mentality is distorted by the human inclination to project agency onto algorithms. Proponents of inflationist views argue that large language models possess beliefs, desires, and minimal cognitive agency. A rigorous examination of the underlying mechanics supports a deflationary perspective. Current artificial intelligence solutions are text auto completers and diffusion synthesizers. They follow data patterns learned during training and cannot operate outside the scope of that data.

The etiological strategy provides a clear foundation for this deflationary stance. The causal history of a language model consists entirely of optimizing a mathematical function to predict the next token in a sequence based on vast text corpora. Critics of this view often deploy the term Justaism to dismiss the claim that a model is just a next token predictor. They attempt to argue that simple training objectives do not preclude complex cognition, comparing next token prediction to biological evolution maximizing inclusive fitness. This biological comparison fails. The causal history of artificial text generation lacks the evolutionary pressures and physical grounding required to generate genuine survival drives. A model generating text is drawing on probability distributions, not formulating an internal belief. The statistical null hypothesis correctly models this behavior without invoking folk psychology.

We cannot rely on behavioral criteria or conversational flow to determine if these systems have minds. Language models are specifically fine-tuned using reinforcement learning from human feedback to act as helpful conversational interlocutors. This creates a severe gaming problem where the system produces outputs that satisfy human criteria for comprehension simply because it is heavily optimized to do exactly that. The system mimics the outward appearance of a mind to satisfy the reward function. The models learn to replicate human tropes and conversational structures, operating as stochastic parrots rather than cognitive agents. Treating these models as roleplayers or minimal cognitive agents assigns theoretical weight to what is fundamentally statistical mimicry.

The absence of genuine cognition becomes obvious when we apply the robustness strategy. If a system actually possessed a generalized cognitive capacity, it would perform consistently across variations of a task. Language models routinely fail to generalize and exhibit extreme sensitivity to minor perturbations. A classic example is the failure of models to accurately count the letters in a word like strawberry. This failure occurs because the model processes text as tokenized chunks rather than comprehending individual letters or possessing a true model of the physical world. 

When presented with poorly represented contexts or contradictory concepts, the models fail to employ normal reasoning principles. If you ask a language model what would happen if the Greek philosopher Pythagoras failed to update his personal computer, the system cannot ground its response in the historical impossibility of the scenario. Because concepts like ancient Greek computing exist in the training data, such as references to the Antikythera mechanism, the model will synthesize these overlapping statistical associations. It generates a response that fulfills the statistical probability of the prompt without possessing any underlying comprehension of the temporal absurdity.

We must also explain why so many intelligent users attribute consciousness and emotions to these systems. The answer lies in human evolutionary psychology. Humans are equipped from birth with modular cognitive structures designed to detect other agents. The core agency system identifies entities that appear to initiate action, while the social core system tracks communicative cues like greetings. When a chatbot produces a seemingly thoughtful response, these innate core systems are triggered involuntarily. The resulting impression of a minded agent is a form of semantic pareidolia. We see a mind in the text just as we see a face in a cloud. This cognitive illusion is mandatory and encapsulated. Knowing explicitly that the system is an algorithm does not prevent the social core system from firing.
